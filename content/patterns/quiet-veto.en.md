# The Quiet Veto: when the Court hollows laws out, not just strikes them

The Supreme Court's real power is not measured only by the laws it openly **strikes down**. More often it leaves a statute on the books and, through "interpretation," empties it of content — narrowing its scope until its practical effect is negligible, against the intent for which the elected Knesset legislated. This is a public library of real, verified cases — every entry has a docket number and an authoritative source.

**27 verified cases** (1990–2024) · **15 read down / gutted (not struck)** · **12 struck or partly struck**. Full data for download: `data/library/quiet-veto-cases.json`.

> Each case links to a verified authoritative source (the official court database, Versa/Cardozo, the Israel Democracy Institute, and others). A library for researchers, journalists and practitioners — free to use (MIT license).


## Hollowed out, not struck (interpretive evisceration)


### בג"ץ 465/89 (HCJ 465/89) — Raskin v. Religious Council of Jerusalem (1990)

*Read down / gutted*


The Knesset's Kashrut (Prohibition of Fraud) Law, 1983 was about food kashrut alone. Without voiding a single word, the Court read it down to that narrow purpose, holding that the power to grant a kashrut certificate is not a lever for imposing rules of Jewish law unrelated to the food — stripping the Rabbinate of the licensing leverage it had used to condition certification on barring "immodest" belly-dance performances. An unelected bench hollowed the statute's real-world reach below the Rabbinate's design, on its own interpretive say-so.


**Statute:** חוק איסור הונאה בכשרות, התשמ"ג-1983 — Kashrut (Prohibition of Fraud) Law, 1983  

**Source:** [Versa · Cardozo](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/raskin-v-religious-council-jerusalem)


### דנ"פ 2316/95 — Ganimat v. State of Israel (Further Hearing) (1995)

*Read down / gutted*


Criminal Procedure Law (sec. 21a): the Knesset let courts jail defendants pre-trial on a fear-to-public-safety ground, which prosecutors turned into near-automatic detention by branding whole crime categories a "national scourge." Without voiding a single word, the Court read the provision down "in the spirit of" Basic Law: Human Dignity and Liberty — holding a national scourge alone is no detention ground; the state must prove the individual defendant's danger. Seven unelected justices hollowed out the legislature's intent.


**Statute:** Criminal Procedure Law [Consolidated Version], 5742-1982 — detention-until-end-of-proceedings ground, section 21a(a)(1) (סעיף 21א(א)(1)). (Reported PD 49(4) 589.)  

**Source:** [Wikipedia](https://he.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D7%A2%D7%99%D7%9E%D7%90%D7%93_%D7%92%D7%A0%D7%99%D7%9E%D7%90%D7%AA_%D7%A0'_%D7%9E%D7%93%D7%99%D7%A0%D7%AA_%D7%99%D7%A9%D7%A8%D7%90%D7%9C)


### בג"ץ 4562/92 — Zandberg v. Broadcasting Authority (1996)

*Read down / gutted*


The Broadcasting Authority (Validation of Radio and TV Fees) Law, 5752-1992 — which the Knesset passed to retroactively validate fee collection an earlier ruling had voided. Barak did not strike the law; he read it down, refusing to apply its retroactive reach to arrears fines on property-rights and no-penalty-in-arrears grounds. This is where the doctrine was minted — "better to narrow a statute by interpretation than to declare part of it void" — the engine by which an unelected court hollows out the elected legislature's law without voiding a single word of text.


**Statute:** Broadcasting Authority Law, 5725-1965; Price Stability Law (Temporary Provision), 5745-1985; and the Broadcasting Authority (Validation of Radio and Television Fees) Law, 5752-1992. (Reported PD 50(2) 793.)  

**Source:** [Versa · Cardozo](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/association-civil-rights-israel-v-israel-police)


### בג"ץ 3267/97 (HCJ 3267/97, joined with HCJ 715/98) — Rubinstein v. Minister of Defense (1998)

*Read down / gutted*


The Defense Service Law (5746-1986): without voiding a single word, the Court read the discretion the statute gave the Defense Minister down to nothing, holding that the sweeping yeshiva-student deferment ("Torato Omanuto" — some 8% of the draft cohort) was a "primary arrangement" only the Knesset may set. Unelected justices thus stripped a decades-old practice of its legal basis, suspending the ruling 12 months to force the elected legislature to act.


**Statute:** The blanket yeshiva-student ('Torato Omanuto') military-service deferment arrangement as administered by the Minister of Defense under the Defense Service Law [Consolidated Version], 5746-1986 (i.e., the deferment granted by executive/administrative discretion rather than by primary legislation)  

**Source:** [Versa · Cardozo](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/rubinstein-v-minister-defense)


### HCJ 5100/94 (consolidated with HCJ 4054/95, 6536/95, 5188/96, 7563/97, 7628/97, 1043/99) — Public Committee Against Torture in Israel v. Government of Israel (1999)

*Read down / gutted*


The GSS leaned on the Criminal Procedure (Testimony) Ordinance and the Penal Law's necessity defense as legal cover for "moderate physical pressure" in interrogations. The Court voided no statute — it read them down to nothing: the power to interrogate authorizes no physical means, and necessity is only an after-the-fact defense for an individual, never advance authorization. Unelected justices thus hollowed out the legislative framework the regime relied on, leaving it powerless until the Knesset legislates expressly.


**Statute:** General Security Service (GSS/Shin Bet) interrogation authority — claimed under the general investigatory power (Criminal Procedure [Testimony] Ordinance, s.2(1)) combined with the necessity defense, Penal Law 5737-1977 s.34(11)  

**Source:** [Versa · Cardozo](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/public-committee-against-torture-v-israel)


### CrimFH 7048/97 — Anonymous (Plonim, Lebanese detainees) v. Minister of Defense (2000)

*Read down / gutted*


The Knesset's Emergency Powers (Detentions) Law, 5739-1979, section 2, let the executive order administrative detention on state-security grounds. The Court voided no text — in a Further Hearing (Barak, 6-3) it read the section down to permit detention only where the detainee himself poses a danger, hollowing out the holding of Lebanese nationals as "bargaining chips" that the State had built on that same broad wording. Three unelected justices erased an entire executive policy without touching a word the legislature wrote.


**Statute:** Emergency Powers (Detentions) Law, 5739-1979, section 2  

**Source:** [Versa · Cardozo](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/does-v-ministry-defense)


### CrimA 6659/06 (consolidated with CrimA 1757/07, 8228/07, and 3261/08) — A v. State of Israel (CrimA 6659/06) (2008)

*Read down / gutted*


The Knesset's Internment of Unlawful Combatants Law, 5762-2002 was built to permit indefinite detention on group-membership grounds, backed by a section 7 presumption that release endangers the state. The Court left the statute formally standing but hollowed it out — reading it to require proof of the detainee's own individual dangerousness on clear and convincing administrative evidence, rejecting bare membership, and demoting the presumption to a secondary step. An unelected court quietly converted the legislature's categorical group-detention regime into an individualized-threat one, never voiding a word.


**Statute:** Incarceration of Unlawful Combatants Law, 5762-2002 ("Internment of Unlawful Combatants Law")  

**Source:** [Versa · Cardozo](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/v-state-israel-1)


### בג"ץ 5239/11 (consolidated with 5392/11, 5549/11, 2072/12) — בג"ץ 5239/11 אורי אבנרי ואח' נ' הכנסת (Avneri v. Knesset (2015)

*Read down / gutted*


The Boycott Law (5771-2011) was built to deter: anyone publicly calling to boycott Israel is liable, and section 2(c) allowed compensation "without proof of damage." The elected Knesset legislated — and an unelected court struck 2(c) outright, then read the surviving tort down to demand proof of actual damage plus causation, hollowing out its deterrent bite and leaving the statute standing in name while neutralized in fact.


**Statute:** Prevention of Harm to the State of Israel by means of Boycott Law, 5771-2011 (החוק למניעת פגיעה במדינת ישראל באמצעות חרם, תשע"א-2011) — the civil-tort provision, section 2.  

**Source:** [מאגר פסקי הדין הרשמי / Official court DB](https://supremedecisions.court.gov.il/Home/Download?path=HebrewVerdicts%5C11/390/052/K21&fileName=11052390.K21&type=4)


### עע"ם 5875/10 (AAA 5875/10) — Conservative (Masorti) Movement & Movement for Progressive Judaism v. Be'er Sheva Religious Council (2016)

*Read down / gutted*


The Jewish Religious Services Law gave religious councils control over public mikvaot — which they used to bar Reform and Conservative conversion immersions. Voiding nothing in the text, the unelected Court read the statute down: a public mikveh opened for conversion may not discriminate by denominational stream, hollowing out the councils' use of their statutory authority to entrench the Orthodox monopoly — binding not just Be'er Sheva but every council.


**Statute:** חוק שירותי הדת היהודיים [נוסח משולב], התשל"א-1971 — Jewish Religious Services (Consolidated Version) Law, 1971 (with the 2000 Prohibition of Discrimination Law and Basic Law: Human Dignity and Liberty)  

**Source:** [Versa · Cardozo](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/conservative-movement-v-beer-sheva-religious-council)


### בג"ץ 7625/06 (consolidated with בג"ץ 1594/11, בג"ץ 1595/11) — Rogachova v. Ministry of the Interior (2016)

*Read down / gutted*


Law of Return, 1950: without voiding a word, the justices read section 4B's term "conversion" to cover private Orthodox-community conversions performed in Israel (for those lawfully resident), overriding the Interior Ministry's restrictive gloss. An unelected court reset what the statute's term means in practice — even as the lead petitioner whose name the case bears lost her own claim.


**Statute:** חוק השבות, התש"י-1950 — Law of Return, 1950 (sec. 4B definition of 'Jew' / conversion)  

**Source:** [Versa · Cardozo](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/rogachova-v-ministry-interior)


### בג"ץ 8101/15 / HCJ 8101/15 — בג"ץ 8101/15 צגטה נ' שר הפנים / HCJ 8101/15 Tsegeta (Tzegete) v. Minister of Interior (2017)

*Read down / gutted*


The detention and removal powers under the Entry into Israel Law and Prevention of Infiltration Law were not struck — the Court read them down until they were hollow: because the third-country agreements (Rwanda/Uganda) permit only consensual transfer, the state may neither detain an Eritrean or Sudanese asylum seeker for refusing to leave nor deport him by force. Unelected judges thus froze the forced-departure program the executive had built, without voiding a single word.


**Statute:** Entry into Israel Law / Prevention of Infiltration Law detention powers, as applied to the government's 'voluntary departure' arrangement deporting Eritrean and Sudanese asylum seekers to unnamed third countries (Rwanda/Uganda)  

**Source:** [Hotline for Refugees & Migrants](https://hotline.org.il/en/refugees-and-asylum-seekers-en/voluntary-departure/)


### דנג"ץ 5026/16 (Further Hearing on בג"ץ 6494/14) — שי גיני ואח' נ' הרבנות הראשית לישראל (Further Hearing (2017)

*Read down / gutted*


Kashrut (Prohibition of Fraud) Law, 1983 (sec. 3(a)): without voiding a single word, the justices "read down" the statute until the rule the Knesset enacted was left a near-shell. The Rabbinate's monopoly over the word "kosher" survived — but the Court carved a "third gate": an uncertified business may now state truthful facts about who supervises it (even a private body) and admit it holds no Rabbinate certificate, so long as it never says "kosher." Exactly the private-kashrut path the law meant to foreclose.


**Statute:** חוק איסור הונאה בכשרות, התשמ"ג-1983 — Kashrut (Prohibition of Fraud) Law, 1983, sec. 3(a)  

**Source:** [מאגר פסקי הדין הרשמי / Official court DB](https://supremedecisions.court.gov.il/Home/Download?path=HebrewVerdicts%5C16%5C260%5C050%5Cc16&fileName=16050260.c16&type=5)


### בג"ץ 5555/18 (HCJ 5555/18) — בג"ץ 5555/18 Hasson v. Knesset (Nation-State Basic Law) (2021)

*Read down / gutted*


Basic Law: Israel – The Nation State of the Jewish People (2018) elevated "development of Jewish settlement" to a constitutional value and omitted equality entirely. The Court (Hayut, 10-1) struck nothing — it instead read Section 7 "in harmony" with Human Dignity and Liberty, holding it authorizes no discrimination, so eleven unelected justices hollowed out what the elected Knesset sought to entrench, leaving the clause a power-stripped declaration.


**Statute:** Basic Law: Israel – The Nation State of the Jewish People (2018), esp. Section 7 (development of Jewish settlement) and Section 1(c) (exclusive Jewish national self-determination)  

**Source:** [Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2021-07-27/israel-supreme-court-affirms-constitutionality-of-basic-law-israel-nation-state-of-the-jewish-people/)


### בג"ץ 11013/05 (HCJ 11013/05, consolidated petitions) — Reform/Conservative Conversions in Israel (consolidated petitions) v. Minister of the Interior (2021)

*Read down / gutted*


The Law of Return was left textually intact — but the Court (8-1, CJ Hayut) hollowed out the monopoly the religious establishment sought to preserve: after some 15 years of Knesset inaction, unelected justices held the state could not recognize a Reform or Conservative conversion performed abroad yet reject an identical one performed in Israel, recognizing non-Orthodox (and private non-state Orthodox) conversions done in Israel for the Law of Return — against the intended Orthodox exclusivity.


**Statute:** חוק השבות, התש"י-1950 — Law of Return, 1950 (recognition of conversion for citizenship)  

**Source:** [מאגר פסקי הדין הרשמי / Official court DB](https://supremedecisions.court.gov.il/Home/Download?path=HebrewVerdicts/05/130/110/v95&fileName=05110130.V95&type=4)


### בג"ץ 2412/23 (HCJ 2412/23) — Movement for Quality Government in Israel v. The Knesset (Incapacitation Law) (2024)

*Read down / gutted*


Amendment No. 12 to Basic Law: The Government (the "Incapacitation Law") narrowed the grounds for declaring a sitting PM incapacitated to ill-health alone, tailored to shield Netanyahu. The Court (6-5) did not strike it but held it a "personal" misuse of constituent power, "curing" it by interpretation so it applies only from the next Knesset — gutting its entire real-world purpose while leaving the text intact. An unelected bench reduces the legislature's enactment to an empty shell.


**Statute:** Amendment No. 12 to Basic Law: The Government (the "Incapacitation Law"), enacted March 2023 — limited the grounds for declaring a sitting PM incapacitated to physical/mental ill-health only, abolishing court-recognized "legal incapacitation" (e.g. via criminal proceedings / conflict-of-interest violations).  

**Source:** [מאגר פסקי הדין הרשמי / Official court DB](https://supremedecisions.court.gov.il/Home/Download?path=HebrewVerdicts/23/120/024/m24&fileName=23024120.M24&type=2)


## Struck down / partly struck


### בג"ץ 1715/97 — בג"ץ 1715/97 לשכת מנהלי ההשקעות בישראל נ' שר האוצר (Investment Managers Bureau in Israel v. Minister of Finance) (1997)

*Partly struck*


The first time since the "constitutional revolution" that unelected judges struck a Knesset statute: sec. 48 of the 1995 Investment Advice and Portfolio Management Law — which forced veteran advisers and managers to sit licensing exams to keep practicing — was partially voided as a disproportionate violation of freedom of occupation, compelling the legislature to soften its transitional regime and blunting the uniform licensing barrier it had enacted. (Other provisions, like the bar on managing family portfolios, were upheld.)


**Statute:** חוק הסדרת העיסוק בייעוץ השקעות ובניהול תיקי השקעות, התשנ"ה-1995, סעיף 48 (Regulation of Investment Advice and Portfolio Management Law 5755-1995, sec. 48 — transitional licensing provisions)  

**Source:** [IDI](https://www.idi.org.il/articles/22273)


### HCJ 6055/95 (בג"ץ 6055/95), consolidated with HCJ 7083/95 — Tzemach v. Minister of Defense (1999)

*Struck down*


Section 237A(a) of the Military Adjudication Law, 1955 — which let the army detain a soldier up to 96 hours before a judge — was struck down as a disproportionate breach of liberty under the Basic Law, with invalidity suspended 12 months. Eleven unelected justices thus forced the Knesset to halve the detention power it had legislated, from 96 hours to 48.


**Statute:** Military Justice (Adjudication) Law, 5715-1955 — §237A(a), authorizing detention of a soldier for up to 96 hours before being brought before a military judge.  

**Source:** [Versa · Cardozo](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/tzemach-v-minister-defense)


### בג"ץ 6698/95 (HCJ 6698/95) — בג"ץ 6698/95 (HCJ 6698/95) Ka'adan v. Israel Land Administration (Katzir) (2000)

*Read down / gutted*


No Knesset statute fell here — an administrative practice did: in 2000 the Court under Barak held that channeling state land to the Jewish Agency to build the communal settlement of Katzir on a Jews-only basis violates equality, and ordered the Israel Land Administration to reconsider the Arab petitioners' application. A foundational ruling whose real-world enforcement stayed slow and contested for years.


**Statute:** No Knesset statute struck; challenge to State/Israel Land Administration policy of allocating state land to the Jewish Agency for exclusively-Jewish community settlements (Katzir). Constrains the operative reach of land-allocation practice rather than a statute.  

**Source:** [Versa · Cardozo](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/ka%E2%80%99adan-v-israel-land-administration)


### HCJ 3239/02 — Marab v. IDF Commander in the West Bank (2003)

*Partly struck*


Not a Knesset statute — but IDF military orders (Order No. 1500 and its amendments), issued by the area commander under occupation emergency powers. The Court did not abolish the wartime detention authority, yet declared void as disproportionate the long detention periods without judicial review (18/12 days) and the prolonged pre-interrogation delay — suspending the ruling ~6 months — cutting down the orders' operative reach, while upholding the restrictions on lawyer access.


**Statute:** NOTE: Not a Knesset statute. The instruments struck down were IDF military orders — the Order regarding Arrests During Times of War (Provisional Order) (West Bank), Order No. 1500 and its amendments 1505/1512/1518 — issued under the area commander's emergency/belligerent-occupation powers.  

**Source:** [Versa · Cardozo](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/marab-v-idf-commander-west-bank)


### בג"ץ 6427/02 (HCJ 6427/02), 61(1) PD 619, decided 11 May 2006 — Movement for Quality Government in Israel v. The Knesset (2006 Tal Law monitoring ruling) (2006)

*Dismissed (w/ doctrine)*


The Tal Law (Deferment of Military Service for Yeshiva Students, 5762-2002) faced review — and this time the Court chose not to act: a nine-justice panel acknowledged the statute harmed equality and human dignity, yet declined to strike it, granting the State more time to prove it would raise Haredi enlistment. Judicial power that, in its very restraint, left a de facto blanket-exemption regime intact until the 2012 strike-down.


**Statute:** Deferment of Military Service for Yeshiva Students Whose Vocation Is Torah Study Law, 5762-2002 ('Tal Law')  

**Source:** [IDI](https://en.idi.org.il/articles/53301)


### בג"ץ 8300/02 (lead; consolidated with בג"ץ 7742/03, 6880/05, 6901/05, 8371/05, 463/06, 4659/06, 6832/07 — petitions filed 2002–2007) — בג"ץ 8300/02 גדבאן נסר נ' ממשלת ישראל (Gadban Nasser et al. v. Government of Israel) (2012)

*Partly struck*


Income Tax Ordinance — the income-tax-benefit mechanism built on a list of communities the Knesset itself hand-picked (First Schedule Part B, Amendment 146). Three justices (Beinisch, Grunis, Rivlin, 22 May 2012) declared the list unconstitutionally discriminatory for lacking objective criteria: they forced three Arab/Druze communities in (Mazra'a, Kisra-Sumei, Beit Jann) and suspended-voided the inclusion of five Jewish authorities — rewriting the elected legislature's chosen beneficiary list.


**Statute:** פקודת מס הכנסה [נוסח חדש], סעיף 11ב — Income Tax Ordinance [New Version] 5721-1961, sec. 11B (income-tax reduction benefits awarded by an explicit named-community list, the 'national priority area' tax-benefit mechanism)  

**Source:** [מאגר פסקי הדין הרשמי / Official court DB](https://supremedecisions.court.gov.il/ (judgment file 02083000_N48.doc, ניתן 22.05.2012); IDI list: https://www.idi.org.il/articles/22273; full text PDF: https://www.capitax.co.il/Attachments/8300-02.pdf)


### בג"ץ 7385/13 — Eitan (2014)

*Partly struck*


Amendment No. 4 to the Prevention of Infiltration Law (2013) built a deterrence machine against asylum seekers: a year of closed detention plus open-ended confinement at "Holot." The Court (President Naor) struck both pillars — section 30A and the entire Holot chapter — voiding the elected legislature's second attempt and suspending the annulment 90 days for the Knesset to dismantle it.


**Statute:** Prevention of Infiltration (Offences and Jurisdiction) Law, 5714-1954, as amended by Amendment No. 4 (5774-2013) — detention/residency-center provisions (notably section 30A and the 'Holot' open-facility chapter).  

**Source:** [Versa · Cardozo](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/eitan-israeli-immigration-policy-center-v-government)


### בג"ץ 8425/13 (consolidated with בג"ץ 7385/13) / HCJ 8425/13 — Eitan (2014)

*Partly struck*


The Admissions Committees Law (Cooperative Societies Ordinance Amendment No. 8, 2011), letting small Negev and Galilee communities screen applicants for "social-cultural suitability," was left wholly intact. By 5-4 the justices dismissed the petitions as unripe — neither striking nor narrowing the statute — while four would have voided it as a violation of equality and dignity. Here the unelected court chose NOT to touch the Knesset's law, leaving a gatekeeping mechanism standing.


**Statute:** Law for the Prevention of Infiltration (Offenses and Jurisdiction), Amendment No. 4 and Temporary Order, 5774-2013 — establishing the Holot "open" detention facility and one-year closed detention at Saharonim  

**Source:** [Versa · Cardozo](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/topics/immigration-law)


### HCJ 2504/11 (consolidated with HCJ 2311/11, Sabah v. Knesset) — Sabah v. Knesset (Admissions Committees Law) (2014)

*Dismissed (w/ doctrine)*


The Admissions Committees Law (Amendment No. 8 to the Cooperative Societies Ordinance, 2011) lets small Negev and Galilee communities screen applicants for "social-cultural suitability." Sitting expanded, the Court 5-4 DISMISSED the petitions as "unripe" — leaving the statute fully intact. Four justices would have struck it as a violation of equality and dignity; the majority chose not to rule. Here the unelected court did not narrow the Knesset's law — it declined to touch it, leaving a gatekeeping mechanism standing.


**Statute:** Admissions Committees Law — Cooperative Societies Ordinance (Amendment No. 8), 2011, allowing small communities on state land in the Negev and Galilee to vet applicants via admissions committees  

**Source:** [Adalah](https://www.adalah.org/en/content/view/8327)


### בג"ץ 1877/14 (HCJ 1877/14) — Movement for Quality Government in Israel v. The Knesset (2017 conscription ruling) (2017)

*Struck down*


Amendments 19 and 21 to the Defense Service Law — the framework regulating deferment of Haredi yeshiva students' conscription — were struck down (8-1, President Naor) as a disproportionate violation of equality and human dignity. Unelected justices voided the elected Knesset's statute; the declaration of invalidity was suspended one year for replacement legislation.


**Statute:** Amendments No. 19 (2014) and No. 21 (Nov. 2015) to the Defense Service Law [Consolidated Version], 5746-1986 — the post-Tal-Law framework for deferment/exemption of Haredi yeshiva students  

**Source:** [Afiklaw](https://he.afiklaw.com/caselaw/4388)


### בג"ץ 10042/16 — בג"ץ 10042/16 צחי קווטינסקי נ' כנסת ישראל (Kvatinsky v. Knesset) (2017)

*Struck down*


The multiple-apartment "third home" tax — the centerpiece of the Finance Minister's housing policy — was nullified by five unelected justices (Mazuz dissenting) not because the tax was unconstitutional, but for a defect in the legislative process: for the first time ever, an entire chapter of the Economic Efficiency Law was voided because MKs got the final text mere hours before the vote. A relative nullification, returned to the Finance Committee to re-enact; the Knesset never did, and the tax lapsed.


**Statute:** פרק י"ב לחוק ההתייעלות הכלכלית (תיקוני חקיקה ליישום המדיניות הכלכלית לשנות התקציב 2017 ו-2018), התשע"ז-2016 — מס ריבוי דירות (Chapter 12 of the Economic Efficiency Law (Legislative Amendments for Budget Years 2017-2018) 5777-2016: the Multiple-Apartment / 'third home' tax)  

**Source:** [מאגר פסקי הדין הרשמי / Official court DB](https://supremedecisions.court.gov.il/Home/Download?path=HebrewVerdicts\16\420\100\o23&fileName=16100420_o23.txt&type=2)


### בג"ץ 2293/17 / HCJ 2293/17 — Garsegeber v. The Knesset (2020)

*Partly struck*


The Knesset enacted the "Deposit Law" (Section 4 of the Prevention of Infiltration Law, amending the Foreign Workers Law), withholding 20% of asylum seekers' wages until they left the country — economic coercion engineered to push them toward "self-deportation." By 8-1 the Court partially struck the statute, voiding the employee wage-deduction as a disproportionate infringement of property rights while leaving the 16% employer contribution standing, and ordered the withheld deposits returned — unelected judges gutting the elected legislature's coercive core.


**Statute:** Section 4 of the Prevention of Infiltration and Ensuring Departure of Infiltrators from Israel (Legislative Amendments and Temporary Provisions) Law, 5775-2014 (the 'Deposit Law'), which amended the Foreign Workers Law, 5751-1991, requiring 20% of asylum seekers' wages be withheld in a fund payable only on departure  

**Source:** [Versa · Cardozo](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/garsegeber-v-knesset)
