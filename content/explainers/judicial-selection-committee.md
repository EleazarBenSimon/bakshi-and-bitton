# The Judicial Selection Committee (הוועדה לבחירת שופטים)

## What it is

The Judicial Selection Committee is the statutory body that appoints all judges in Israel — Supreme Court, District, and Magistrate. It is established under *Basic Law: The Judiciary* and operates per the Courts Law and supplementary regulations.

Unlike most democracies, where senior judges are appointed by the elected executive (often with legislative confirmation), Israel's Committee gives sitting judges and the Bar Association significant institutional weight in selecting their colleagues and successors. This composition — and the supermajority rule that operates on top of it — is the structural mechanism around which most of the 2023-2025 reform debate turned.

## Composition (as of mid-2026)

The Committee has **nine members**:

| Members | Source |
|---|---|
| 3 Supreme Court justices | Court itself; includes the Supreme Court President |
| 2 Bar Association representatives | Israel Bar Association |
| 2 Ministers | Cabinet (one is the Justice Minister, who chairs) |
| 2 Knesset Members | Knesset (by convention, 1 coalition + 1 opposition) |

This composition has been adjusted in different ways over the years, but the **3-judge / 2-bar / 4-political** balance has been the long-standing default.

## The supermajority rule for Supreme Court appointments

Since a 2008 reform, **Supreme Court appointments require a supermajority of 7 out of 9 members**. This means:

- The 3 sitting justices, voting together, **plus any one additional member** (a Bar rep, an MK, or a minister), can block any appointment they oppose
- Equivalently, no Supreme Court appointment can succeed against the unified opposition of the sitting justices

The supermajority rule was, at the time of its 2008 enactment, presented as a measure to ensure broad consensus on appointments. In practice, it has functioned as something closer to a **veto power for the sitting Court over its successors** — at least when the sitting justices vote as a bloc.

This is the structural feature that Cohen-Eliya and other reform-aligned scholars identify as the operating mechanism of what they call the "juristocracy": the Court's institutional capacity to perpetuate its own composition through the supermajority gate.

## The seniority convention for Court President

By convention (not statute), the **most senior sitting justice** becomes the next President of the Supreme Court upon the prior President's retirement. The Committee formally elects the President, but in practice the choice is determined by seniority.

This convention came under stress in 2023-2025 when Justice Minister Yariv Levin refused to convene the Committee for ~16 months after Esther Hayut's October 2023 retirement, in order to block the elevation of Yitzhak Amit (next in seniority).

The High Court of Justice ultimately ordered the Committee convened, and Amit was elected President in January 2025 — with the coalition members boycotting both the vote and the subsequent swearing-in ceremony. See `data/rulings/` for related documentary entries.

## The March 2025 reconstitution law

On **27 March 2025**, the Knesset passed a law restructuring the Committee's composition:

- The 2 Bar Association representatives were removed
- 2 new positions were added: 1 lawyer chosen by the coalition, 1 lawyer chosen by the opposition

This shifts the balance toward political control of the Committee, though less aggressively than the original January 2023 Levin-Rothman proposal (which would have given the coalition automatic majority).

The new law takes effect at the start of the **next** Knesset — meaning it doesn't apply to current Committee operations but will reshape future appointments.

## Why this is the central post-2024 reform battleground

After the January 2024 Supreme Court ruling striking down the reasonableness amendment, the broader reform package was effectively dead. The coalition pivoted from direct constraint of the Court's powers to **changing who sits on the Court** through Committee restructuring.

The logic is the same logic that Cohen-Eliya identifies in [*Israel's Juristocracy*](https://lawliberty.org/israels-juristocracy/): the Court's authority and the Committee's composition are mutually reinforcing. The Court's authority over Knesset legislation depends on the Court's composition; the Committee's composition controls the Court's composition; the supermajority rule gives the Court itself a veto over the Committee's composition decisions.

Breaking this circuit, in the reform-aligned view, is necessary to restore democratic accountability to the appointment of judges. In the defenders' view, breaking this circuit risks turning the Court into a partisan instrument and undermining judicial independence.

## A note on adversarial fairness

Defenders of the existing supermajority structure argue that:
1. Judicial independence requires insulation from majoritarian politics
2. Supermajority requirements ensure broad consensus across political shifts
3. Removing the Bar reps (in the 2025 law) reduces professional input in favor of pure politics

The reform-aligned position, articulated by Cohen-Eliya, Bakshi, Koppel, and Rothman, holds that:
1. Independence from politics is not the same as immunity from democratic legitimacy
2. The supermajority "consensus" requirement in practice gives the sitting Court an over-representative voice in its own succession
3. Bar Association representation gives outsized weight to a professional guild whose interests do not necessarily align with broader public preferences

This explainer documents the structure and the debate; it does not adjudicate which position is correct.
