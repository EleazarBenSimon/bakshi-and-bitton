# Constituent Authority and Basic Laws (סמכות מכוננת וחוקי-יסוד)

## Why this concept matters

Israel does not have a single written constitution. Instead, the Knesset, since 1958, has passed a series of **Basic Laws** (חוקי-יסוד) covering different aspects of governmental structure and rights — currently 13 in total. The intention, per the 1950 Harari Resolution, was that these would eventually be combined into a complete written constitution.

The Basic Laws have a special status: they are passed as ordinary statutes, but the Israeli Supreme Court has treated them as constitutional-rank texts, holding ordinary legislation that conflicts with them as invalid. This means the Knesset, when it enacts a Basic Law, is treated as acting in two roles simultaneously — as ordinary legislator AND as **constituent assembly** (רשות מכוננת) creating constitutional norms.

The legal-doctrinal foundation for this dual role was established in 1995. The extension of judicial review to **amendments of Basic Laws themselves** was established in 2024.

## The 1995 foundation: Bank Mizrahi

In [**CA 6821/93 United Mizrahi Bank v. Migdal Cooperative Village**](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/united-mizrahi-bank-v-migdal-cooperative-village) (9 November 1995), the Supreme Court — sitting as a nine-justice panel for what was nominally a civil appeal about agricultural debt — articulated the foundational doctrine.

President Aharon Barak's lead opinion held:

- The Knesset acts in **two capacities**: as ordinary legislator (passing statutes) and as **constituent assembly** (creating Basic Laws of constitutional rank)
- This dual capacity transferred from the First Knesset, which Harari Resolution authorized as a constitution-drafter, to **every subsequent Knesset** through institutional continuity
- The Basic Laws of 1992 (Human Dignity and Liberty; Freedom of Occupation), and their **limitation clauses** (§8 and §4 respectively), provide the textual authorization for judicial review of ordinary legislation
- The source of constituent authority is **popular sovereignty** rather than parliamentary sovereignty

This was a substantial doctrinal pronouncement. As Cohen-Eliya notes in [*Israel's Juristocracy*](https://lawliberty.org/israels-juristocracy/), Barak's framework "radically expanded the significance of the 1992 Basic Laws, far beyond any intention contemplated by the legislators who voted for them." Barak's argument, in his earlier 1976 formulation that he later operationalized as President, was that judges are "the vanguard of the aspiration for the better, more desirable law... the architects of social change."

### A critical detail often missed

The Bank Mizrahi ruling — despite being the foundation of all subsequent constitutional review of ordinary legislation in Israel — **did not actually strike down any law**. The Court upheld the Family Agricultural Sector (Arrangements) Law Amendment that was challenged. The doctrinal architecture (Knesset's constituent authority, Basic Laws as supreme constitution, judicial power to invalidate statutes) was articulated *in obiter dicta* — pronouncements made in the course of the judgment but not strictly necessary to its disposition.

The first actual statutory invalidation under the Bank Mizrahi framework came two years later, in *Investment Managers Bureau* (1997).

Whether the Bank Mizrahi pronouncement counts as binding holding or as persuasive dicta is itself a battleground in the post-2023 debate. Defenders treat it as the ratio of a 30-year-stable jurisprudence; critics (Cohen-Eliya, Ben Shemesh) treat it as a judicial self-bootstrapping that subsequent precedent has papered over.

## The 2024 extension: judicial review of Basic Law amendments

Bank Mizrahi authorized the Court to strike down **ordinary legislation** that conflicts with Basic Laws. The 2024 ruling extended this to **Basic Law amendments** themselves.

In [**HCJ 5658/23**](https://supremedecisions.court.gov.il/Home/Download?path=HebrewVerdicts%2F23%2F580%2F056%2Fv31&fileName=23056580.V31&type=2) (1 January 2024), the Court — sitting *en banc* as a 15-justice panel — issued two distinct rulings:

- **12-3**: The Court has authority, in principle, to invalidate amendments to Basic Laws that exceed the Knesset's constituent authority
- **8-7**: Amendment No. 3 to Basic Law: The Judiciary (the "reasonableness amendment") is invalidated as exceeding that authority

President Hayut's lead opinion adopted a framework analogous to India's "basic structure" doctrine: the Knesset's constituent authority is not unlimited, and amendments that deny "the core characteristics of the State of Israel as a Jewish and democratic state" are reviewable.

## What this means structurally

The combination of Bank Mizrahi (1995) and HCJ 5658/23 (2024) gives the Israeli Supreme Court an unusual position by comparative-constitutional standards:

1. **The Court reviews ordinary legislation** against Basic Laws (since 1995)
2. **The Court reviews amendments of Basic Laws** against doctrinal limits the Court itself articulates (since 2024)
3. **The Court reviews appointment to the Court** through the Judicial Selection Committee's supermajority rule (since 2008)
4. **The Court reviews executive decisions** under the broadly-construed reasonableness doctrine (since the 1980s)

Cohen-Eliya argues in *Israel's Juristocracy* that this combination is **"no precedent in the democratic world"** — that no other democratic system has accrued comparable judicial power without explicit constitutional authorization. India and Bangladesh have analogous basic-structure doctrines, but both operate within fully codified constitutions. Israel's doctrine operates without one.

## A note on adversarial fairness

Defenders of the constituent-authority framework — Barak himself, Daphne Barak-Erez (now a sitting justice), and scholars like Yoav Dotan — argue:

1. The limitation clauses (§8 of Human Dignity and Liberty; §4 of Freedom of Occupation) **textually authorize** judicial review of legislation; without judicial enforcement, the clauses are meaningless
2. The Knesset's own legislative practice — passing Basic Laws under more demanding procedural rules, citing them as constitutional norms in subsequent debates — implies self-understanding as constituent assembly
3. The basic-structure doctrine extended in 2024 has parallels (India, Bangladesh, Slovakia) and is necessary to prevent constitutional abuse via simple-majority Basic Law amendments

The reform-aligned position, articulated by Cohen-Eliya, Ben Shemesh, and Kontorovich, holds:

1. The 1992 Basic Laws passed with **tiny majorities** (Basic Law: Human Dignity and Liberty cleared with just 32 of 120 MKs present) — inconsistent with the claim that the Knesset self-understood itself as enacting a constitution
2. The Bank Mizrahi framework is **judicial self-assertion** — the Court declaring its own authority without explicit textual or democratic mandate
3. The 2024 extension to Basic Law amendments is **without precedent in any democratic system that lacks a codified constitution**; the comparator democracies all have codified constitutions providing the authorization

This explainer documents the framework and the debate; it does not adjudicate which position is correct.
