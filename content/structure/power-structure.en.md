This chapter is not about a single ruling but about a **structure**: a set of procedural mechanisms through which decision-making power moved from elected officials to an unelected layer of judges and legal advisors. Each mechanism is presented in two parts — **what already happened** (the documented outcome it produced), then **the mechanism** (how it actually works, and its legal basis). The procedural facts are verifiable from the law and from how the system operates; the judgment that this amounts to a concentration of power is the project's assessment, anchored in the disclosed facts beside it.

---

## 1. The apex court as the court of first instance — and last

**What already happened.** On 18 January 2023 the High Court of Justice voided, 10–1, the appointment of Aryeh Deri as Minister of the Interior and of Health, holding it "unreasonable in the extreme." Prime Minister Netanyahu said he was "compelled, with a heavy heart" to dismiss him, and did so by cabinet vote days later. The government had no avenue of appeal: the HCJ is both the first and the last judicial word. *(The ruling, in this database: HCJ 8948/22 Deri.)*

**The mechanism.** Section 15 of Basic Law: The Judiciary provides that the Supreme Court also sits as the **High Court of Justice**, hearing petitions against state authorities directly. The same body is thus both the state's final court of appeal and the first-instance forum where a citizen challenges a minister's or government's decision — with no appeal from its decision. In every other democracy with judicial review of executive action, the first-instance ruling can be appealed to a higher court that corrects error and moderates a single bench's preferences. In Israel the HCJ **is** the Supreme Court: the government faces a single adjudication, before the most powerful bench in the country, with no further legal recourse. The Court did the same on 1 January 2024, when a full bench of 15 justices reviewed and struck down an amendment to a Basic Law (see §7) — also at first and last instance.

## 2. Direct access to the apex — bypassing the lower courts

**What already happened.** In August 2019, after the justice minister fired the ministry's director-general and moved to install a political associate, a petition was filed **directly with the HCJ**, and within days the appointment was frozen by interim order. No administrative tribunal or district-court step preceded it, and no appeal was possible — the HCJ was the only forum from the first moment. *(Source: Jerusalem Post and Haaretz reporting, August 2019.)*

**The mechanism.** Petitions on the most significant national matters — government decisions, regulations, appointments, legislation — go straight to the Supreme Court. The 2000 Administrative Courts Law moved enumerated categories to district courts, but everything not expressly listed stays with the HCJ via a catch-all clause. In most legal systems a challenger must exhaust remedies, pass a specialist tribunal, a general court, and an appellate tier — and the government governs throughout. In Israel, for the retained categories, there is no exhaustion requirement and no lower-court filter: an appointment announced Monday can be frozen by the HCJ by Friday. The government cannot use procedural time as a political resource — which also amplifies the Attorney General's power (§3), since the threat of rapid intervention makes the AG's opinion costly to ignore.

## 3. The chain of legal advisors — binding opinions, not advice

**What already happened.** In 2025 the Attorney General announced she would not represent the government in petitions concerning the Prime Minister's refusal to establish a state commission of inquiry into the events of 7 October, and that the government would have to seek its own private counsel. The government's own legal arm opposed its position, and the Prime Minister had to find private lawyers to appear in the country's highest court — against his own Attorney General. *(Source: Times of Israel and Ynet reporting, 2025.)*

**The mechanism.** The Attorney General's binding authority has **no explicit statutory basis** — it was built from AG Shamgar's 1968 self-declaration that the role is "of a judicial nature," crystallized in Justice Barak's 1993 Deri-Pinhasi ruling that the AG's opinion "reflects the positive law," and operationalized through government resolutions. To this is added a **monopoly on representation**: the AG decides what arguments the State of Israel presents in court. If the AG deems the government's position "indefensible," the government's only options are to abandon it, or to appear in its own name with private counsel — with no legal apparatus, against its own AG. Ministry legal advisors — professionally subordinate to the AG, not to the minister — can refuse to draft an opinion supporting a decision they deem unlawful, and the minister cannot dismiss them. The entire structure — granting an appointed official a veto over the government's legal strategy — rests on judicial interpretation and government resolutions, not on a statute the Knesset enacted.

## 4. Panel composition — who decides which justices hear the case

**What already happened.** HCJ 5658/23, in which an amendment to a Basic Law was struck down, was heard by an **expanded panel of 15 justices** — and decided 8 to 7. The size of the panel determined the outcome: on a smaller panel the majority might have flipped.

**The mechanism.** Assignment is computerized by default, but the **Court President holds sole discretion over panel size** — expanding it "to any odd number" in matters of particular importance. As academic critique notes (Federalist Society Review), choosing seven justices instead of five can decide a charged constitutional case. An empirical study (Givati & Rosenberg, *Journal of Empirical Legal Studies*, 2020) found that justices a sitting President had earlier selected sit on her panels more often — consistent with strategic composition. Outcome-affecting discretion in the most sensitive cases is thus concentrated in one office. *(This is not a claim that the President hand-picks every panel; the default is random, and the effect runs through panel size and the exception.)*

## 5. No standing requirement — anyone, on almost anything

**What already happened.** In 2006 the HCJ struck down the "Tal Law" (military-service deferment for yeshiva students) on a petition by the **Movement for Quality Government** — a civic NGO whose members suffer no personal injury from the deferment arrangement. Legislation backed by a governing coalition was voided on the petition of a body with no personal stake. The same arrangement was struck down again and again afterward (2012, 2017, 2024), each time on petitions by organizations with no personal injury. *(Source: HCJ 6427/02; VERSA, Cardozo.)*

**The mechanism.** In HCJ 910/86 Ressler v. Minister of Defence (1988), Justice Barak reduced the standing requirement almost to nothing and created a parallel track: even without personal injury, standing is granted where the matter is of "particular public importance" and touches the rule of law. As VERSA notes, the Court "effectively removed [standing] as a threshold." The result is structural: organized, well-funded organizations can use the HCJ as a continuous policy-review mechanism against any government — and the parliamentary majority provides no shield, because the Court itself decides whether standing is met.

## 6. Judicial self-selection — and the case the project is named after

**What already happened.** Justice Minister Yariv Levin sought to appoint to the Supreme Court **Aviad Bakshi** and **Raphael Bitton** — two figures identified with the 2023 reform. Lacking the required supermajority (7 of 9) for those appointments, a **deadlock** set in: the minister declined to convene the committee to appoint other justices, and three Supreme Court seats stood vacant for an extended period, contributing to a severe judge shortage across the court system. *(The deadlock was two-sided: both the minister's refusal to convene and the committee members' votes against the candidates produced it. Sources: Times of Israel; IDI.)* This project's name — **Bakshi & Bitton** — is taken from this episode.

**The mechanism.** The Judicial Selection Committee has nine members, three of them sitting Supreme Court justices. Since 2008 a **supermajority of 7 of 9** is required to appoint Supreme Court justices. The asymmetry is structural: **blocking** a candidate takes only five votes; **appointing** one takes seven. Over time this draws the Court's composition toward candidates acceptable to the sitting justices, not necessarily to the elected government of the day — the layer reproducing itself and diminishing elected officials' weight in its composition. *(In 2025 the supermajority structure was changed by contested legislation, now deferred and under constitutional review by the Court itself.)*

## 7. The Court defines the limits of its own jurisdiction — "everything is justiciable"

**What already happened.** On 1 January 2024 the Court, sitting as a full bench of 15, struck down Amendment No. 3 to Basic Law: The Judiciary — the amendment that sought to remove reasonableness review of elected-branch decisions. By 12–3 it held, **in principle**, that it has authority to review amendments to Basic Laws; by 8–7 it voided this specific amendment. The Knesset's attempt to narrow the Court's review power was thus annulled by the Court itself, under a theory of jurisdiction the Court devised for itself, with no avenue of appeal or override. *(The ruling, in this database: HCJ 5658/23.)*

**The mechanism.** Justice Barak's "everything is justiciable" doctrine, together with the reversal of the "political question" doctrine in Ressler (1988), expanded review so that almost any matter — including policy, security, and appointment decisions once treated as "political" — is open to judicial review. The structural feature: **there is no external body that can tell the Court it lacks jurisdiction.** The Court decides what is justiciable, and its justiciability rulings are not themselves reviewable. It is a one-way ratchet: the Court expanded justiciability, and when the Knesset tried to narrow it by statute, the Court voided the narrowing under its self-declared power to review even Basic Laws.

## A note on method

The descriptions of the mechanisms above are **procedural facts**, verifiable from the legal basis, the case law, and how the system operates; each outcome is given with its source. The judgment that this amounts to a concentration of power and a weakening of elected government is the project's **assessment** — an opinion anchored in the disclosed facts beside it, not a factual claim about any individual's state of mind or motive. We have deliberately avoided the sweeping comparative claim ("nothing like it anywhere"): the structural fact itself carries the argument. The figures named here are named solely in connection with their public, documented acts in their official roles.
