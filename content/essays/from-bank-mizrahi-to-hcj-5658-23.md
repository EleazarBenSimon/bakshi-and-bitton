# From Bank Mizrahi to HCJ 5658/23 — A Documentary Record of Thirty Years of Judicial Expansion

## What this essay does

This essay traces the constitutional-doctrinal arc of the Israeli Supreme Court from a foundational 1995 ruling to a peak moment in January 2024 — a thirty-year period during which the Court substantially expanded its own authority over Israel's elected institutions. The empirical record is drawn from this project's documentary core (`data/rulings/`) and from sources cited throughout.

The interpretive frame is Prof. Moshe Cohen-Eliya's juristocracy thesis as articulated in [*Israel's Juristocracy*](https://lawliberty.org/israels-juristocracy/) (Law & Liberty, March 2024) and in the broader work of the constitutional-conservatism scholars he is most closely associated with (Iddo Porat, Aviad Bakshi, Moshe Koppel, Yaakov Ben Shemesh). The opposing view, anchored in Ran Hirschl's *Towards Juristocracy* (Harvard, 2004) and in defenders of the constitutional-revolution architecture, is engaged in adversarial fairness.

This is a documentary essay. It traces what happened, with citations. It does not attempt to settle the question of whether the developments documented were normatively good or bad. That question is properly decided in democratic deliberation; what this essay provides is the structured record of what is to be deliberated.

## The starting point: a constitution that was never written

Israel was founded in 1948 with the intention of adopting a written constitution. The Harari Resolution of 1950 deferred that work: rather than drafting a single document, the Knesset would pass a series of "Basic Laws" (חוקי-יסוד), one or a few at a time, that would eventually be combined into a complete constitution.

For four decades, this approach proceeded slowly. Basic Laws were passed on the structure of state institutions — the Knesset itself, the government, the judiciary, the President — without anyone treating them as a constitution. They were ordinary statutes about constitutional subjects, not constitutional norms with supra-legislative status.

This was the institutional baseline that the Israeli Supreme Court inherited.

## The 1992 inflection: Basic Laws on rights

In March 1992, the Knesset passed two Basic Laws focused on individual rights: **Basic Law: Human Dignity and Liberty** and **Basic Law: Freedom of Occupation**. Both included "limitation clauses" — §8 and §4 respectively — articulating the conditions under which the protected rights could be infringed.

Cohen-Eliya, in *Israel's Juristocracy*, notes the political circumstances:

> "a bi-partisan coalition of representatives for the declining Ashkenazi-secular-liberal group acted in concert to ensure the constitutional entrenchment of rights that conformed to their interests and value system."

The legislative passage itself was thinly attended. Basic Law: Human Dignity and Liberty cleared with **just 32 of 120 MKs present** — a fact later invoked in the debate over whether the Knesset, at that moment, self-understood itself as enacting a constitution rather than ordinary legislation.

For three years, the implications of the 1992 Basic Laws remained latent. Then came Bank Mizrahi.

## 1995: The doctrinal foundation (Bank Mizrahi)

In [**CA 6821/93 United Mizrahi Bank v. Migdal Cooperative Village**](https://versa.cardozo.yu.edu/opinions/united-mizrahi-bank-v-migdal-cooperative-village) (9 November 1995), a nine-justice panel of the Supreme Court — hearing what was nominally a civil appeal about agricultural debt — articulated the doctrine that would define the next three decades.

Justice Aharon Barak's lead opinion held that:

- The Knesset acts in **two capacities**: as ordinary legislator and as **constituent assembly** (creating Basic Laws of constitutional rank)
- This dual capacity transferred from the First Knesset (which the Harari Resolution authorized as a constitution-drafter) to every subsequent Knesset through institutional continuity
- The 1992 Basic Laws and their limitation clauses provide textual authorization for judicial review of ordinary legislation
- The source of constituent authority is **popular sovereignty** rather than parliamentary sovereignty

There was a critical procedural detail. **Bank Mizrahi did not actually strike down any law.** The Court upheld the challenged amendment to the Family Agricultural Sector (Arrangements) Law. The doctrinal architecture — Knesset's constituent authority, Basic Laws as supreme constitution, judicial power to invalidate statutes — was articulated *in obiter dicta*, pronouncements made in the course of the judgment but not strictly necessary to its disposition.

The first actual statutory invalidation under the Bank Mizrahi framework came two years later, in *Investment Managers Bureau* (1997).

This procedural detail matters for the present debate. Defenders of the Bank Mizrahi framework treat the constitutional-review architecture as a settled holding, ratified by thirty years of subsequent precedent. Critics treat it as **judicial self-assertion** — a court declaring its own authority without explicit textual or democratic mandate, and 30 years of compounding precedent then making the original self-assertion harder to revisit.

Cohen-Eliya's diagnosis:

> "There is no precedent in the democratic world for a legal system accruing such vast powers, with no explicit statutory authorization and no clear mechanism conferring popular legitimacy."

The defenders' counter, articulated by Barak himself and by sympathetic scholars, holds that the limitation clauses are textually unintelligible without judicial enforcement: someone must apply them, and that someone is necessarily a court.

## The first two decades after Bank Mizrahi: doctrinal entrenchment

Between 1995 and roughly 2018, the Bank Mizrahi framework was applied incrementally. The Israel Democracy Institute's empirical work (Amir Fuchs and colleagues) counted approximately **22 laws or legal provisions invalidated** under the framework through 2020. The pace was modest — averaging less than one strikedown per year — but each instance entrenched the framework further.

Equally consequential, however, was the **parallel doctrinal expansion** of administrative review. Under Barak's tenure (1995-2006) and his successors, the Supreme Court substantially broadened:

- **Standing** — the threshold question of who can bring a petition. Under Barak, the traditional standing requirements were largely eliminated; any citizen could petition on matters of public interest.
- **Justiciability** — the question of which issues are appropriate for court review at all. Many issues that had previously been treated as political (and therefore not justiciable) became subject to review.
- **Reasonableness** — see the dedicated explainer in `../explainers/reasonableness-doctrine.md`. The narrow Wednesbury standard was replaced by a broader "balancing" version under which judges could second-guess discretionary executive choices.
- **The role of the Attorney General** — through judicial rulings of the 1990s, the AG's directives became binding on the government, and the AG was granted monopoly over government legal representation in court.

By the late 2010s, the cumulative effect of these expansions was an institutional architecture in which the Court — together with the AG's office it had structurally empowered — held effective last-word authority over an unusually broad range of executive and legislative decisions.

## The post-2022 acceleration

The 2022 Knesset elections returned Likud and its religious-Zionist and Haredi partners to government, with Justice Minister Yariv Levin and Constitution Committee Chair Simcha Rothman presenting in January 2023 a package of reforms intended to roll back several elements of the post-1995 expansion. The package included an override clause, restructuring of the Judicial Selection Committee, abolition of the "reasonableness" doctrine for cabinet/PM/minister decisions, and a bar on judicial review of Basic Law amendments.

The reform package generated mass protest. By July 2023, only one element of the original package had been enacted: Amendment No. 3 to Basic Law: The Judiciary, removing reasonableness review of cabinet/PM/minister decisions. The amendment was challenged immediately. The Court heard the case as an *en banc* 15-justice panel on September 12, 2023.

In the period between oral argument and the issuance of the ruling, the war that began on October 7, 2023 transformed the political context. Two of the justices on the panel — Chief Justice Esther Hayut and Justice Anat Baron — reached mandatory retirement age in October 2023, but used the statutory three-month post-retirement window to participate in the ruling's delivery.

## 2024: The peak moment (HCJ 5658/23)

On **January 1, 2024**, the Supreme Court issued [HCJ 5658/23](https://supremedecisions.court.gov.il/Home/Download?path=HebrewVerdicts%2F23%2F580%2F056%2Fv31&fileName=23056580.V31&type=2). The full structured record is in `../../data/rulings/5658-23.json`.

Two distinct holdings, voted separately by the same 15-justice panel:

- **12-3**: The Court has authority, in principle, to invalidate amendments to Basic Laws that exceed the Knesset's constituent authority.
- **8-7**: Amendment No. 3 to Basic Law: The Judiciary is invalidated as exceeding that authority.

Hayut's lead opinion adopted a framework analogous to India's basic-structure doctrine: the Knesset's constituent authority is not unlimited, and amendments that deny "the core characteristics of the State of Israel as a Jewish and democratic state" are reviewable.

Cohen-Eliya's framing of the ruling, written two months after it:

> "This ruling represents a peak moment of judicial activism on a global scale... in the wake of the events on October 7th, such a hyper-activist judicial ruling raises serious concerns regarding democratic accountability in a legal system in which the judiciary holds unprecedented powers."

The structural significance is twofold:

First, the Court extended its review authority from ordinary legislation (since Bank Mizrahi 1995) to **Basic Law amendments themselves**. The democratic-authorization argument for this extension is substantially weaker than for ordinary legislation, because Basic Laws are the closest thing Israel has to a constitution — and amending them through democratic process is the way constitutional change is supposed to happen in any democracy.

Second, the Court **preserved its own most-used doctrinal tool** (reasonableness review of cabinet decisions). The pattern document in `../patterns/court-intervention-pattern-2014-2026.md` shows that reasonableness was the doctrine in 6 of 9 documented cases — making its preservation existentially significant for the Court's continued intervention capacity.

## 2025-2026: The post-2024 acceleration

After the January 2024 ruling, the reform agenda's direct path was effectively closed. The coalition pivoted to two parallel strategies:

1. **Restructure the Judicial Selection Committee** — see `../explainers/judicial-selection-committee.md`. The March 27, 2025 reconstitution law removed Bar Association reps and added political appointees, though it takes effect only in the next Knesset.

2. **Block individual Court appointments through the supermajority gate** — Justice Minister Levin refused to convene the Committee for 16 months to block Yitzhak Amit's elevation to Court President. The High Court ordered the Committee convened; Amit was elected with coalition members boycotting; PM Netanyahu, Justice Minister Levin, and Speaker Ohana boycotted the swearing-in.

During the same period, the Court continued to intervene in elected-branch appointment decisions:

- **Baharav-Miara dismissal** (Dec 2025): seven-justice panel unanimously struck the cabinet's first-ever vote to fire an Attorney General. See `../../data/rulings/18225-06-25-baharav-miara.json`.
- **Zini / Shin Bet** (Dec 2025): three-justice panel split 2-1; majority declined to intervene; Court President Amit in dissent argued the appointment committee should examine the Prime Minister's own conflict of interest. See `../../data/rulings/427-10-25-zini.json`.
- **Gofman / Mossad** (May 2026): three-justice panel unanimously remanded the appointment on procedural grounds — applying logic substantively similar to Amit's Zini dissent. See `../../data/rulings/gofman-mossad-2026-05.json`.

The Court's intervention rate in high-profile appointment decisions during this period is documented in `../patterns/court-intervention-pattern-2014-2026.md`.

## Synthesis: what the thirty years show

Taken together, the period from 1995 to 2026 documents a recognizable trajectory:

1. **A self-asserted doctrinal foundation** (Bank Mizrahi 1995) treating the Court as the enforcer of constitutional norms the Knesset is held not to have democratically debated as such
2. **A two-decade entrenchment** during which the Bank Mizrahi framework was applied incrementally and parallel doctrinal expansions (standing, justiciability, reasonableness, AG authority) accumulated
3. **A failed reform attempt** (2022-2024) to roll back portions of the framework
4. **A peak doctrinal moment** (January 2024) in which the Court not only blocked the reform but extended its review authority to constitutional amendments themselves
5. **A post-2024 acceleration** in which the Court has continued to intervene in high-profile elected-branch appointment decisions while the reform agenda pivots to Committee restructuring

Cohen-Eliya's juristocracy framing, drawing on Hirschl, holds that this trajectory is the operating signature of a declining hegemonic group transferring power from elected institutions (which it can no longer control) to unelected institutions (in which it retains influence). The framing is contested — defenders argue the trajectory reflects necessary constitutional development in a system without other checks — but the empirical record of doctrinal expansion is not seriously in dispute.

## A note on Hirschl's adversarial fairness

Ran Hirschl, in *Towards Juristocracy* (Harvard, 2004), articulated the comparative-constitutional framework that Cohen-Eliya later applied to Israel: hegemonic preservation, the transfer of authority from elected to judicial institutions, the structural logic of declining groups using courts to lock in their interests.

Crucially, Hirschl's own work is **descriptive-critical, not prescriptive-restorationist**. He identifies the pattern without endorsing the political conclusion that the pattern should be reversed. As of mid-2026, Hirschl has not publicly endorsed the 2023-2024 Israeli reform effort despite his theoretical framework being its most-cited intellectual foundation. He has remained, in the public discourse, unaligned with either side of the political fight that uses his theory.

This is significant for the project: the theoretical framework can hold both for those who argue the documented trajectory should be reversed (Cohen-Eliya, Bakshi, Koppel, Ben Shemesh) and for those who treat it as a stable feature of Israeli democracy that should be defended (Hayut, Barak-Erez, Dotan). The documentary record this essay traces is the same in both cases.

## Where the documentary record goes next

This essay traces thirty years of doctrinal arc. The story continues — the 2025-2026 cluster of Court interventions in elected-branch appointments, the Selection Committee restructuring effects in the next Knesset, the still-pending reform bills, the next major test cases.

The documentary core of this project, and its informative content layer, will continue to record what happens. Subsequent essays in this directory will treat sub-themes in more depth: the AG's role, the Selection Committee mechanic, the pattern of appointment-based intervention, the doctrinal differences between Justice Sohlberg's and President Amit's votes on similar facts.

What this essay establishes is the historical baseline against which all of that future record can be read.
